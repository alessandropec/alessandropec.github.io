<p class="ale-project-lead">A survey connecting the psychological foundations of human memory with the computational architectures used to give virtual agents continuity, adaptation and more coherent long-term behaviour.</p>

Memory is often discussed as a single capability, even though an artificial agent may need very different mechanisms to retain recent observations, personal experiences, factual knowledge, procedures and spatial relationships. The survey establishes a shared structure for discussing these components across psychology, virtual humans and contemporary AI systems.

## What the survey connects

The study follows memory from cognitive concepts—working, episodic, autobiographical, procedural, semantic and spatial memory—to their implementation in virtual humans and agents. It compares how systems encode experience, retrieve relevant information, forget or consolidate content and evaluate whether memory improves behaviour rather than simply increasing stored context.

## A design framework

The resulting framework highlights the interaction between external environments, sensory registers, a central executive and several forms of long-term memory. This creates a common vocabulary for comparing symbolic, neural and LLM-based systems that are otherwise described with inconsistent terminology.

## Context and collaboration

The review was developed within the **Computer Graphics and Vision Group at Politecnico di Torino** as part of research on virtual humans, cognitive architectures and long-term agent behaviour.
